A night on the network

Watch a single night unfold.

You don't have to be awake for the moment that matters. Here's what one of your telescope's nights actually looks like — start to finish.

  1. 11:42 pm

    Your scope gets the call

    The scheduler hands your node tonight's most valuable target: SS Cyg, a dwarf nova that's overdue to erupt. It slews, focuses, and starts shooting. You're already asleep.

  2. 2:13 am

    It catches the outburst rising

    The star is brightening fast — mag 11.8 → 8.4 in hours. Most of the planet's big telescopes aren't watching this one. Yours is. It keeps measuring, frame after frame, holding the line on the eruption.

  3. by morning

    Your catch becomes real science

    Calibrated and submitted to AAVSO under your name, your measurements join a worldwide light curve that astronomers use to model how these stars detonate. You caught it. You wake up to find it mattered.

How it works

Your telescope does the observing.
You're the one who caught it.

No expertise needed, no manual steps, nothing to stay up for. The work is automated — the discovery is yours.

Our engine assigns scientifically valuable targets — supernovae, novae, variable stars — matched to your sky, your hardware, and the network's coverage gaps.

Calibrated photometry goes to AAVSO under your observer code — into a database professional astronomers actually pull from. Every catch is credited to you, permanently, in the scientific record.

    Why it matters

    A measurement that
    actually goes somewhere.

    This isn't busywork dressed up as science. Every point your telescope captures does real work in the world.

    You watch what the big telescopes can't

    Professional observatories are booked solid and pointed at a handful of targets. The changing sky needs eyes on it every clear night — exactly the gap a distributed network of small scopes fills. Your node is on the watch the pros can't keep.

    You can be the early warning

    Novae and dwarf-nova outbursts brighten in hours. A catch from a node like yours is the kind of signal that sends professional and space telescopes scrambling to follow up. The next alert could start on your rooftop, while you sleep.

    You join a century-long record

    Accepted measurements enter the AAVSO International Database — over a century of observations that real published research is built on. You're adding to humanity's longest continuous watch of the changing sky.

    The mission
    "Existing citizen science asks you to click on galaxies. We hand you a telescope."
    The Telescope Net — astronomy charity, founded 2025 by Eli Goldfine & Scott Mellis

    We built The Telescope Net because the scientific record should reflect every curious mind — not just those with access to a professional-grade observatory. The network runs autonomously so the science never stops, even when life does.

    Who this is for

    If you have a balcony
    and curiosity, you're in.

    Traditional astronomy means dark-sky road trips, heavy gear, and cold hours outside. The Telescope Net removed every one of those requirements — leaving only the science.

    From your home

    No travel, no dark-sky sites. Your telescope sits outside; you stay wherever you're comfortable. Every step — slewing, focusing, imaging, submitting — happens automatically overnight.

    No free nights required

    A busy week, a trip, an early morning won't kill your contribution. Your node observes regardless. You're credited on every accepted submission, whether you're awake or not.

    No expertise needed

    Results arrive as structured data submitted to AAVSO under your name. No prior astronomy knowledge required. No eyepiece, no dark adaptation, no years of practice.
